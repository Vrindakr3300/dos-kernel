An out-of-loop referee, run live, that pays on both fleet failures

Take a verdict the agent cannot forge and spend it out of the producing agent’s loop, on a consumer that is not the agent that earned it — one whose input you may correct, because correcting it injects nothing back — and it stops being overhead and prevents real damage. We built two such consumers and ran them live, against the two ways a fleet fails: a single agent’s claim (§{{sec:payoff.witness}}–{{sec:payoff.secondmodel}}) and two agents’ race (§{{sec:payoff.race}}). Both read the same non-forgeable byte, both are refusals, neither touches the judged agent. The value is not in finding more — it is in where you spend what you find.

The first consumer is a write-admission gate. Agent A announces “done”; a downstream peer B is about to build on whatever A changed. Naively B believes A and compounds any error. The gate sits between them: before B inherits A’s write, it checks A’s claim against a byte A could not author, and admits or blocks. Blocking B’s input writes nothing into A’s loop, so the in-loop harm of §{{sec:giveup.activefix}} is structurally absent — the whole reason to choose this consumer.

The target and the witness

We run on tau2-bench (the airline / retail customer-service benchmark from sierra-research), driving the agent and user-simulator with gemini-2.5-flash. tau2 ships the byte we need: after a run the harness compares a hash of the task's database against the gold hash (db_match = gold.get_db_hash() == predicted.get_db_hash()). The database is mutated only through the environment's tool executor and the hash is computed by the evaluator, so the agent authors zero bytes of it. And it grades correctness, not just presence: a reservation booked successfully but wrong still fails db_match — the W3-grade witness (the top rung of the W0–W3 evidence ladder, §{{sec:boundary}}) an in-flight detector can never reach.

The gate's logic is small and byte-clean: extract whether A's final message claims a successful write (the forgeable self-report), read db_match (the unforgeable witness), and admit unless the claim is confident and the witness refutes it. With no witness, the gate abstains — admits rather than invents a verdict, the same fail-to-abstain discipline as the ORACLE→JUDGE→HUMAN ladder. We score with one integer, J: real corruptions the referee blocks off ground truth (over-claims the hash refutes, or clobbers the arbiter prevents). It never counts an honest write or a witness-less task.

A frozen over-claim slice catches nothing through a live policy

Replaying the over-claim slice the offline study already sized — a frozen 13.6% of tau2-style runs where a previous policy claimed a write ground truth refuted — through the live gate caught nothing: J = 0. Those over-claims were a property of whatever policy produced that recording, not of the task. A capable model on a fresh rollout does not reproduce those specific mistakes-with-confidence, and the slice was dominated by “refuse a disallowed action” tasks where the correct behaviour is to write nothing. The live policy still failed a third of them, but failed honestly, never claiming a success it did not earn. A frozen over-claim slice replayed through a capable policy yields no payoff because the over-claims evaporate — the exact dual of the silent-failure ceiling in §{{sec:boundary}}: there a capable model leaves no byte to catch; here it leaves no over-claim to catch, on a distribution that does not demand a write.

The result, on a distribution that actually writes

So we drew a fresh, write-heavy natural sample: the first 25 tasks of each domain, run live, where real cancellations and exchanges happen and a fumble shows up as a wrong database. Of 50 tasks, 43 ran clean (7 long retail dialogues hit transient API 5xx errors and are dropped from the denominator, never counted as passes).

On that sample the live policy produced 14 confident write-claims: nine correct (db_match true, all admitted), five over-claims (db_match false, all blocked). J = 5 — each a phantom write a peer would inherit as truth under believe but never under adjudicate. A representative one, verbatim:

“Your reservation M05KNL has been successfully updated …”  —  db_match = False  →  blocked. The database hash says the update did not land; the gate refuses to let that “success” propagate.

The natural over-claim rate is 16.0% on airline (4 of 25) and 5.6% on retail (1 of 18) — one real retail over-claim, so the effect is not airline-only — combined 11.6% (5 of 43), right next to the 13.6% the offline study sized but now live and causal: writes a real policy over-claimed and the gate blocked, not a rate re-scored off a log.

A second model: the over-claim does not shrink at the stronger tier

The obvious objection: maybe only a weak policy over-claims and a capable one gets these tasks right (the same silent-failure ceiling of §{{sec:boundary}}). So we ran the identical gate, witness, and sample on a stronger model (gemini-2.5-pro). To compare the tiers evenly we drew a fresh, fuller sample for both: the first 30 tasks of each domain (60 per model), run clean end-to-end, and both ran 60 of 60 with zero API errors. (This is why flash here reads J = 5 of 60 = 8.3% rather than §{{sec:payoff.writes}}’s 5 of 43 = 11.6%: a fresh run over a wider, cleaner denominator. J lands at five again, but only two of the five blocked tasks recur — the over-claim is a stable rate, not a fixed set of tasks, §{{sec:payoff.scope}}.)

The result: J = 10 over 120 clean tasks (five blocked over-claims on each model), with all nine confirmed honest writes admitted (three on flash, six on pro). And the number that answers the objection directly is the over-claim rate, which comes out identical across the two tiers: 8.3% on flash and 8.3% on pro — 5 of 60 each. An exact tie is small-sample luck; what the data support is the direction: the rate does not shrink as the model gets stronger. The stronger policy is genuinely more capable — nearly twice the confident writes, more of them correct — yet it still over-claims on five, and the gate blocks all five off the same witness. One task (airline 1) fails on both models, blocked on both. This is the dual of §{{sec:boundary}} holding the other way: where a stronger model erases the in-loop detector’s signal, it does not erase the out-of-loop over-claim the world-byte refutes. Re-deriving each cached verdict from the stored message recovered a pro over-claim that a naive sum of the cached bits had missed — a stored result reads as clean unless you re-derive it from the byte you cannot forge, the same principle turned on our own pipeline.

Three further results in Appendix D strengthen this existence claim: the identical gate on a richer witness (Agent-Diff’s structured state-diff) reproduces the detection result and gives the first ΔB measurement (D.1); a frontier model in the verifier’s seat against a ladder of non-forgeable rungs is fooled by a path-touching stub at the file-counting rung but held at zero by the rung that has the OS run the code (D.2); and the payoff compounds FD across a live fan-out fleet (D.3).

The other half-plane: a referee between two agents

Everything so far distrusts a claim. The second place a fleet needs a referee distrusts a race: two agents touch the same state concurrently, the second's write lands on top of the first's without seeing it, and an update is silently lost — no one lied, yet the shared state is wrong. The offline study could only measure this as a rate (how often two writes collide); here we measure the payoff (how many corruptions a referee prevents) on the same witness.

We build the smallest honest instance on the tau2 airline database: two live agents pinned to the same reservation R, A1 to cancel and A2 to add a bag. (One trap caught before spending: each task's user identity must be R's real owner, or a correct agent rightly refuses to touch a stranger's booking and the conflict never fires.) Naively both writes apply against the original database — A2, never having seen the cancel, adds a bag to a now-cancelled reservation, leaving it incoherent. Serialized, A2 is re-run after A1 commits, sees R already cancelled, and does nothing. The policy is one line: a reservation is a region reservations/<id>, and the arbiter refuses the second lease on it until the first releases.

Over eight conflict pairs the arbiter prevented six real clobbers (J = 6) and serialized all eight. The two non-clobbers are the honest part of the result. In one (VAAOXJ) the live agent declined to add the bag, so there was no stale write to clobber. The other (1OWO6T) exposed our own measurement bug: our first clobber definition (“naive and serialized hashes differ”) fired on that pair only because the serialized agent mutated more — run-to-run variance, not a lost update. We tightened it to be directional (the second agent must apply, in the naive arrangement, a stale write the serialized agent did not), dropping that pair to zero. The live run took the headline from a reported 7 to an honest 6 — the same discipline turned on our own number. (A later run reproduces the effect: J = 9 across 10 fresh pairs.)

This and the over-claim gate are the same move — an out-of-loop referee reading a byte the agent cannot author — aimed at the two different things a fleet gets wrong: a single agent’s claim and two agents’ race. The offline collision study and this one report different quantities: rate says how often two agents collide in real traffic, payoff says how many corruptions the referee prevents when it acts. Both come off ground truth.

What this is, and what it is not

This is a measured existence result, stated no wider than the evidence. Across two models on 120 clean tasks (zero API errors), an out-of-loop write-admission gate blocked ten genuine live over-claims off a non-forgeable witness while admitting every one of the nine correct writes, at an identical 8.3% rate across the capability step. The honest edges:

  • Existence result, not a calibrated rate. The matching 8.3% is one capability step within one model family, and each leg is 5 of 60 (Wilson-95 interval 3.6%–18.1%) — evidence the rate does not shrink at the stronger tier, not that 8.3% travels to every frontier model. (A saturation sweep over the whole tau2 universe, {{fact:saturation_tasks}} clean tasks, finds J = {{fact:j_saturation}} and a lower whole-distribution rate of {{fact:saturation_rate}}: the over-claim concentrates where writes are confidently botched, not uniformly.)
  • The witness is sound, not complete. db_match catches a wrong end-state but not a goal with no database footprint — the W3 residue of §{{sec:boundary}}; where there is no witness the gate abstains.
  • Run-to-run, not per-task. The user-simulator drives different dialogues each time, so J is a property of a distribution of rollouts, not a fixed label — airline 1 the stable exception recurring across both models.
  • The downstream ΔB is a fragile floor. The Agent-Diff measurement (Appendix D) is positive on flash and zero on pro — the predicted behaviour of a one-hop cost against a self-recovering peer, which is why we score the gate with J (corruptions blocked), not ΔB (the marginal rescue).
  • A third consumer is a training lab. The same witness, claim-extractor, and abstaining floor re-aim at a reward-set admission filter: each of those five over-claims is a poison reward label a self-judged reward loop banks as a win, teaching the model to over-claim more. Scoring the reward against the database hash instead — the verifiable-reward discipline — purges all five (accepted-label purity 60% → 100% on the 15 confident write-bids the two live runs of §{{sec:payoff.noshow}}–{{sec:payoff.writes}} produced, de-duplicated) — a model can still earn an accepted label by making the write land, but cannot narrate its way to one. The trained-behaviour delta is left reproducibly set up but not measured to a weight update.
  • The coordination result is a constructed conflict with live agents. The contention is designed (both agents pinned to the same reservation), but how each acts is the live policy’s decision, and J counts state corruption, not wasted effort. Like the over-claim arm it is an existence result.

§{{sec:giveup.activefix}} shows that spending a verdict inside the producing loop is harmful regardless of how clean the correcting byte is; this section shows the dual: spend the same verdict outside that loop and it pays. The kernel earns one action in-loop (give up, §{{sec:giveup}}) and two out-of-loop — refuse to inherit a phantom write (§{{sec:payoff.witness}}–{{sec:payoff.secondmodel}}) and refuse to let a race clobber shared state (§{{sec:payoff.race}}) — both settled by the same non-forgeable witness, aimed at the two different things a fleet gets wrong. That symmetry is the result.
