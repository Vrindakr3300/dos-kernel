The boundary that forces it: why the same verdict is harmful in-loop

§{{sec:payoff}} spent the verdict out of the producing loop because the alternative does not work: handed back in, the same verdict is flat-to-harmful. By “in-loop” we mean where the verdict is spent, not how it was measured. To measure it honestly we ran a live A/B (EnterpriseOps-Gym, real Gemini against the gym's hidden database verifiers) — you cannot replay an intervention into a recording and watch the agent react. §{{sec:giveup.activefix}} reports that bake-off: every active fix that writes a turn back into the judged loop (warn, rewind, feed the environment's own correction) came back flat or net-negative. So “detect, not fix in-loop” is a finding we measured, not a feature we ran out of time to build; the one in-loop action that paid off was the negative one (§{{sec:giveup}}).

Why an active fix is so hard shows in what slips past the detectors. The dominant residual failure is confidently-wrong content: the agent finishes with no error and no loop, having never re-read the state it claims to have changed — it says “done” about a world it never checked, and nothing inside the trajectory gives it away. To catch that you need a byte the agent did not write: you have to go look at the real world again after the fact. That is exactly the rung Toolathlon’s own evaluation/main.py oracle occupies — which is why the work splits into two tiers. Tier A is cheap in-loop detection (the three detectors, read offline off the recording), supporting one safe action, the give-up of §{{sec:giveup}} (whose payoff is measured live, because a withheld turn changes what the run spends). Tier B is the live, paid, out-of-loop re-read against a fresh third-party byte — the headline of §{{sec:payoff}}, spent on a downstream consumer’s input rather than back in the judged loop. There is a reason we cannot do this on one benchmark. A detection rate needs a large frozen corpus you can score for free, but an intervention payoff (in-loop or out) can only be measured by changing a live run and watching what happens, which a recording physically cannot show. So Toolathlon sizes the ceiling, and the two live benchmarks measure what acting on a verdict is worth on each side of the loop.

One refinement makes the ceiling sharper. A verifier's rung is graded: the weakest witness is the agent's narration (W0), then the environment acknowledging a write (W1), then a check the change persisted (W2), then a full diff against the gold answer (W3). DOS's own check — "did the change show up in git?" — is W2: it confirms presence, not correctness. And about 38% of frontier goals reach no sound witness at any rung — their correct end-state leaves no checkable invariant, so short of a human no fresh byte settles whether the agent was right. That floor, below even Tier B, marks the honest edge of how far any detector can reach.

Detection in-loop is cheap, precise, and bounded, yet every attempt to act on it in-loop is flat-to-harmful. Both facts push the same way: take the verdict the agent cannot forge and spend it out of that agent’s loop, where acting on it costs the producing run nothing. The next two sections supply the evidence: the one in-loop action that survives (§{{sec:giveup}}), and the byte-clean detector whose precise, narrow signal makes the ceiling measurable (§{{sec:bench}}).
